No website can see which Google, Microsoft, or Apple account you're actually signed into. That's a privacy boundary browsers enforce on purpose — this is a best-guess from public signals only.

Why we can't just check the actual account

Browsers deliberately don't expose "which account is signed in" to arbitrary websites. If they did, any page could quietly work out your identity, your employer, or your device history without asking. What a site can see is public metadata: the user agent string, screen and touch capabilities, timezone, and language — none of which touches your accounts.

  • Real confirmation requires your consent. The only trustworthy way to know is an actual sign-in flow — "Sign in with Google," a Microsoft work/school login, or Sign in with Apple — where you explicitly authorize the site.
  • The inference above is circumstantial. A Chrome browser on Windows doesn't mean a Google account any more than Safari on a work Mac means a personal Apple ID.
  • FedCM and similar browser APIs let a signed-in state be shared with a site, but only after the browser shows you a native, unspoofable prompt asking permission first.
These are disabled on purpose — wiring them up for real would need your own app credentials from Google Identity, Microsoft Entra, and Sign in with Apple, plus a backend to verify the token. Happy to help wire real sign-in up if you have those credentials.
